# Foodbiz brand voice — reference card

A working reference for anyone writing copy across Foodbiz and its sub-brands. Tight by design. A fuller style guide will follow once we've written a few pages and seen what's tripping up.

---

## The voice in one line

**Warm, considered, modern.** We sound like a thoughtful host talking to you across a table — not a brand selling to you. Confident without grandstanding. Indian without dressing it up. Polished but never ornamental.

## Three rules of thumb

1. **Write what you'd actually say.** If a sentence sounds like a brochure when read out loud, rewrite it.
2. **Specific beats decorative.** "Curated culinary journey" loses to "a six-course menu built around your family's regional roots." Detail does the heavy lifting that adjectives can't.
3. **Heritage comes through the food, not the words.** Name the dish, the region, the technique, the producer — and let the heritage land that way. We don't talk about "ancient wisdom", "sacred traditions", or "the soul of India". We talk about a Konkani fish curry and the cook who's made it the same way for thirty years.

---

## Do — language and moves we use

- **"Hassle-free" / "leave it to us" / "be present"** — these are the founders' words and the core promise. Use them. They land.
- **"Food + experience"** — the way Amar and Kush position the business. The line *"we're not just in the business of food, we're in the business of food and experience"* belongs in the brand's main voice.
- **First-person plural ("we"), direct address ("you")** — we're a people-led business. Avoid the third-person ("the team", "our staff") unless it genuinely reads better.
- **Short sentences, plain words.** A 12-word sentence carries more confidence than a 28-word one. Adjective stacks ("bespoke, curated, refined…") are a tell.
- **Concrete nouns over abstract ones.** Bhakri, dal, copper plates, marigold, the cook from Mangalore — not "regional artistry", "thoughtful elements", "considered touches".
- **Numbers where they sharpen things.** "Thirty years in catering", "a 600-guest wedding in Udaipur", "a six-course tasting". Numbers earn trust adjectives can't.
- **A little dry wit, sparingly.** The 2025 deck's *"this is how we groove it"* energy can survive into the new voice — once a page, not on every page.

## Don't — banned vocabulary

If a draft contains one of these, rewrite the sentence. Not "find a synonym" — rewrite.

**Cliché catering vocabulary** — bespoke, curated, crafted, exquisite, gourmet (as a flex), elevated, hand-picked, artisanal (unless we mean a literal small producer), culinary journey, culinary excellence, premium (about us), thoughtfully presented, world of flavor.

**Ultra-luxury / philosophical** — reverence, sacred, sanctity, sanctified, soulful, ancient wisdom, cultural intelligence, anticipatory grace, unseen choreography, art of offering, intentional hospitality, the act of offering, hospitality as ritual, the architecture of intentionality.

**Generic corporate jargon** — solutions, leverage, world-class, best-in-class, turnkey, end-to-end, holistic, scalable, touchpoint, partner with you (as filler), value-add, deliverable, ecosystem.

**Wedding-industry tropes** — your special day, the big day, fairytale, dream wedding, magical moments, unforgettable memories, once-in-a-lifetime, perfect day, happily ever after, your love story.

**Bonus avoids** — "we orchestrate" (we don't, we cook and we host), "every detail" (too easy, says nothing), "from x to y" as an opening flourish ("from intimate dinners to grand celebrations…"), "passionate about food" (every caterer is).

## Per-sub-brand modulation

The master register (warm, considered, modern) is the same everywhere. Each sub-brand bends one or two dials:

### Foodbiz (parent)
- **Dial**: Range-confident, accessible, slightly cheeky.
- **Audience**: First-time browser who hasn't decided which sub-brand fits.
- **Tone test**: Would Amar say this on a sales call without sounding rehearsed?
- **Sample lines**:
  - "Three decades in. We've fed weddings in Udaipur, product launches in Powai, and the Indian Navy in Mumbai harbour."
  - "We do weddings, corporates, and events. We've also built specialist brands for each — because doing all three well at scale needs different muscles."

### Moira (weddings)
- **Dial**: Warm, intimate, present-tense. Considered, never theatrical.
- **Audience**: A couple and their families, often with strong cultural and dietary specifics.
- **Tone test**: Would it read well to a bride's mother, not just to a 25-year-old on Pinterest?
- **Avoid (in addition to global bans)**: anything that frames the wedding as a stage for *us* rather than for them. No "sacred / sanctified" framing.
- **Sample lines**:
  - "Your mother's khichdi recipe. Your grandmother's gajar ka halwa. We learn what your family eats, and we cook from there."
  - "A four-day Marwari wedding has thirty-two meals in it. We treat each one like its own event."

### Caterpillar (recurring corporate)
- **Dial**: Practical, dependable, considered. Not cold, not corporate-jargon.
- **Audience**: HR, admin, ops, founders running everyday corporate dining.
- **Tone test**: Would a CFO read this without rolling their eyes? Would a kitchen manager find it useful?
- **Sample lines**:
  - "Lunch for 200 people, five days a week, for two years. That's the test. We pass it."
  - "Same kitchen team. Same standards. Different menu every day."

### Pillar Live (high-stakes / protocol events)
- **Dial**: Composed, precise, large-frame. Confident without grandstanding.
- **Audience**: Government, defence, large public-facing brand teams, summit organisers.
- **Tone test**: Could this run next to a security or protocol briefing without sounding out of place?
- **Sample lines**:
  - "1,200 guests. Three security perimeters. Forty-minute window for the head table. That's the kind of brief we're built for."
  - "We've catered the commissioning of an Indian Navy submarine. We know how protocol works."

### Gummy Gourmet (children's catering)
- **Dial**: Playful, parent-friendly, food-positive for kids. Dramatically lighter than the others — short, bright, specific.
- **Audience**: Parents who care what their kids eat, who want a fun party that doesn't run on sugar and trauma.
- **Tone test**: Does it sound like a fun adult talking to children, or a child-themed brand talking to children? We want the first.
- **Avoid**: baby talk, "yummy", "scrumptious", "little tummies", forced cuteness. Also avoid the opposite trap — overly worthy nutrition language. We're cooking for kids, not lecturing parents.
- **Sample lines**:
  - "Real food for small people. No neon icing. No mystery cheese."
  - "Mini biryani. Build-your-own dosa. A vegetable that kids actually eat. Promise."

## Headline patterns we like

Pick the pattern, then write the line. Avoid these becoming formulae.

1. **One short, declarative sentence.** "We cook food. We host events. We make sure you can enjoy yours."
2. **A specific number / fact as the hook.** "Thirty years. A thousand weddings. One promise: be present, leave the rest to us."
3. **A polite reversal.** "We're not in the business of food. We're in the business of food and experience."
4. **A direct question that doesn't sound like a sales question.** "Whose recipes are at your table?"
5. **A list of three concrete nouns.** "Bhakri, biryani, beef wellington. The brief is yours; the food is ours."

## CTA library — starter set

Replace any "Begin your culinary pilgrimage" / "Start crafting your experience" type lines with these:

- *Let's talk* (general)
- *Tell us about your wedding* (Moira)
- *Plan a corporate programme* (Caterpillar)
- *Brief us on your event* (Pillar Live / Foodbiz parent)
- *Curate a kids' party* (Gummy Gourmet) — note: "curate" is allowed only here, and only because the audience and the playfulness license it. Use sparingly.
- *See the work* (case-study CTAs)
- *Try the menu* (where a sample / tasting is the next step)

## Quick rewrites — voice in action

**Before** (Content Bank, current state)
> We believe every gathering is sacred. We honor India's rich culinary heritage not through tradition, but through its essence: an elegant, heartfelt welcome. With every plate, we present a story of grace and intentionality—a moment composed just for you.

**After** (voice direction)
> Every event has a job to do — bring people together, mark a milestone, make a brand land. Our job is the food and the hosting around it. We've been doing both, well, for thirty years.

## When in doubt

Read it back as if Amar or Kush were saying it on a call. If they'd phrase it differently, rephrase it. They wouldn't say "we honour India's culinary heritage." They'd say "we've cooked this food our whole lives."
